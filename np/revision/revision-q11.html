🔢 Permutations & Number Formation

11. How many four-digit numbers can be formed from the 9 digits : 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9 such that

(a) there are no repetition of the digits
(b) they are lesser than 4000
(c) they are even numbers greater than 7000?
📝 Fundamental Assumption:
When a problem asks to form numbers "from the given digits" and part (a) explicitly states "no repetition", it is standard practice to assume that we only possess one of each digit for the entire problem. Therefore, the "no repetition" rule applies to parts (b) and (c) as well. Order matters when forming numbers, so we use Permutations ($^{n}P_{r}$).

🅰️ Part (a): No repetition of the digits

We need to fill 4 positions (Thousands, Hundreds, Tens, Units) using the 9 available digits.

_ _ _ _
$$ \text{Total ways} = ^{9}P_{4} = 9 \times 8 \times 7 \times 6 = \mathbf{3,024} $$

Answer: 3,024 numbers

🅱️ Part (b): Lesser than 4000

For a 4-digit number to be less than 4000, the Thousands digit must be 1, 2, or 3. (3 possible choices).

  • Thousands slot: 3 choices.
  • Remaining 3 slots: We have 8 digits left to fill 3 positions ($^{8}P_{3}$).
$$ \text{Ways} = 3 \times (^{8}P_{3}) = 3 \times (8 \times 7 \times 6) $$ $$ \text{Ways} = 3 \times 336 = \mathbf{1,008} $$

Answer: 1,008 numbers

🅲 Part (c): Even numbers greater than 7000

This part has two conflicting restrictions:

  • Greater than 7000: Thousands digit must be $\in \{7, 8, 9\}$
  • Even number: Units digit must be $\in \{2, 4, 6, 8\}$

Because the digit 8 is in both requirement sets, we must split the problem into two mutually exclusive cases to avoid counting the '8' twice.

Case 1: The number starts with 8

  • Thousands slot: Must be 8 (1 choice).
  • Units slot: Must be even. Since 8 is already used, we can only use $\{2, 4, 6\}$ (3 choices).
  • Remaining 2 slots: 7 digits left to fill 2 positions ($^{7}P_{2}$).
$$ \text{Ways for Case 1} = 1 \times 3 \times (^{7}P_{2}) = 1 \times 3 \times (7 \times 6) = 3 \times 42 = 126 $$

Case 2: The number starts with 7 or 9

  • Thousands slot: Must be 7 or 9 (2 choices).
  • Units slot: Must be even. Since we didn't use 8 at the front, we have all even digits available $\{2, 4, 6, 8\}$ (4 choices).
  • Remaining 2 slots: 7 digits left to fill 2 positions ($^{7}P_{2}$).
$$ \text{Ways for Case 2} = 2 \times 4 \times (^{7}P_{2}) = 8 \times (7 \times 6) = 8 \times 42 = 336 $$

Total Ways

Add the possibilities from both cases together:

$$ \text{Total Ways} = \text{Case 1} + \text{Case 2} $$ $$ \text{Total Ways} = 126 + 336 = \mathbf{462} $$

Answer: 462 numbers
